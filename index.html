The article you just read only told you one story.

Every publication has an editorial position. Every author has a point of view. Readers encounter contested claims daily — in news, in opinion, in academic preprints — with no indication that the claim is disputed or that a substantial counter-argument exists.

Wikipedia offers static dispute banners across 40 categories. They are passive, infrequent, and apply only within Wikipedia. Nothing comparable exists across the open web.

Content-Verify treats this as a reading infrastructure problem, not an AI novelty. It extends the critical apparatus that careful readers already apply — marginalia, fact-checking, source comparison — and makes it available at the moment of reading, on any page.

It works the way annotation has always worked.

The extension runs in the background. There is nothing to configure. When you load a page, it reads alongside you.

I.

Detection

The extension identifies 3–4 claims on the page where significant disagreement exists across the scholarly or editorial record. It does not mark every contested sentence — only those where the dispute is substantive.

II.

Marking

Contested text is underlined in place, with color intensity indicating the degree of dispute — pale for moderate disagreement, deeper for strongly contested positions. The original text is unchanged and fully readable.

III.

Analysis

Hovering a marked phrase opens a panel with the principal arguments on each side, drawn from the existing record of debate. The reader decides what to do with that information.
